## What worked best

Three patterns earned more than they cost. I would do all three again, harder, on the next launch.

**1. {WHAT_WORKED_1_HEADLINE_PLACEHOLDER — likely "Receipts-driven content beat conceptual essays by a wide margin" or similar.}**

The receipts-driven posts — the ones where I ran `/validate` against a real OSS repo and published the verdict, the self-validation result of 6/6 PASS with 0 fix attempts, the actual `e2e-evidence/` directory listings — drove disproportionately more attention and conversion than the conceptual essays they were paired with. {WHAT_WORKED_1_SPECIFIC_EXAMPLE_PLACEHOLDER — name the highest-performing receipt post and its top-line metric, e.g. "The {repo-name} demo on {date} drove {N} stars and {N} qualified DMs in the 48h window."}

The lesson generalizes beyond this campaign: in dev tool launches, the demo IS the marketing. Not "the demo supports the marketing." The demo is the entire argument. Everything else is connective tissue.

**2. {WHAT_WORKED_2_HEADLINE_PLACEHOLDER — likely something about LinkedIn long-form outperforming X for consulting conversion, or the Mon-Thu cadence holding, or the blog-series drumbeat.}**

The Mon/Thu LinkedIn long-form cadence held without burning the audience for the full ten weeks. {WHAT_WORKED_2_SPECIFIC_EXAMPLE_PLACEHOLDER — cite the highest-engagement long-form post and its impression count, e.g. "The {post-title} post on {date} cleared {N} impressions and triggered {N} DMs."} The X threads were more efficient per minute of authoring time, but every consulting conversation that turned into a real engagement started in a LinkedIn DM, not an X DM. The two channels do different jobs and the campaign worked because both jobs got done.

**3. {WHAT_WORKED_3_HEADLINE_PLACEHOLDER — likely "The honest retrospective post in Week 3 made the consulting CTA in Week 4 work" or similar.}**

The most counterintuitive call I made was running the honest "what didn't work" retrospective in Week 3 — right after the launch spike, when instinct said to keep the win narrative going. {WHAT_WORKED_3_SPECIFIC_EXAMPLE_PLACEHOLDER — cite engagement on the Week 3 retro and the DMs that arrived in the following week, e.g. "The retro drew {N} substantive comments and consulting DMs the following week spiked to {N}, the highest of the campaign until Week 9."}

The retro was not a brand risk. It was the credibility deposit that made the Week 4 consulting magnet land instead of bouncing off as another sales post. People buy from someone who has admitted to being wrong about specific things.

## What didn't work

Three things landed flatter than I expected. The lessons are sharper than the wins.

**1. {WHAT_DIDNT_WORK_1_HEADLINE_PLACEHOLDER — likely a specific post or channel that under-converted, e.g. the r/programming drop, or one of the middle-week blog adaptations, or the Show HN timing.}**

{WHAT_DIDNT_WORK_1_NARRATIVE_PLACEHOLDER — one paragraph: what happened, why it underperformed, what the specific data is, e.g. "The Post 4 (iOS SSE bridge) LinkedIn adaptation drew {N} impressions, the lowest of the 10-week window. The post was excellent technically but the audience overlap with my LinkedIn followers was thin — most of them are not iOS engineers."} The lesson: niche-depth posts belong on niche-depth platforms, not on the general LinkedIn feed. The next time I have a post that lives 80% in one technical sub-community, it ships to that sub-community first and only excerpts to LinkedIn after the niche audience has had a week with it.

**2. {WHAT_DIDNT_WORK_2_HEADLINE_PLACEHOLDER — likely the blog-series cross-post strategy (Dev.to, Medium) under-delivering, or the Discord drops fading after Week 4, or one Reddit sub bouncing.}**

{WHAT_DIDNT_WORK_2_NARRATIVE_PLACEHOLDER — one paragraph: cite specific cross-post numbers, e.g. "The Dev.to cross-posts collectively drew {N} views across 8 posts. By comparison, a single LinkedIn long-form drew {N} impressions in 24 hours."} I overestimated cross-posting as a way to extend the life of a post. The right move would have been spending that effort on a second original X thread per post — one for launch day, one for day 4, with different angles — rather than cloning the same content across three platforms.

**3. {WHAT_DIDNT_WORK_3_HEADLINE_PLACEHOLDER — likely the second Show HN attempt, or the Product Hunt launch, or one of the consulting-magnet posts not converting at the rate the engagement suggested.}**

{WHAT_DIDNT_WORK_3_NARRATIVE_PLACEHOLDER — one paragraph: what underperformed and the specific gap, e.g. "The Week 8 Code Tales Show HN attempt landed at {N} points, well under the front page threshold. The launch had momentum on every other channel but HN did not pick it up — likely because the project is a content product, not an infra product, and HN's appetite for content products is structurally lower."} The lesson: HN is not a general-purpose distribution channel. It is a specific audience with specific tastes and the second submission paid the price for not understanding that taste.

## What surprised me

Two things I did not see coming.

**1. {SURPRISE_1_PLACEHOLDER — likely something about which content type drove the most consulting DMs, or which audience segment showed up unexpectedly, or which companion repo over-performed relative to its blog post, e.g. "The {repo-name} repo drew {N} stars in 72 hours despite the underlying post being one of the lower-engagement posts of the series. The repo had a more searchable name than the post; people found the repo first and read the post second."}**

**2. {SURPRISE_2_PLACEHOLDER — likely something about the consulting inbound mix, or an unexpected channel like a podcast invitation or a partnership inquiry, or a competitor reaching out. e.g. "Of the {N} qualified consulting DMs received, {N} came from people who had never engaged publicly with any of the content. The inbound was much quieter than the outbound suggested."}**

Both surprises point at the same thing: the visible engagement and the actual conversion are weakly correlated. The post you can feel performing in the comment thread is not always the post that fills your DM inbox the following week. I would have under-weighted that lesson without ten weeks of data to look at side by side.

## What I am doing next

Three forks coming out of the campaign.

**ValidationForge V1.5 — the CONSENSUS engine.** Same self-validation discipline V1 was built under: the engine has to validate itself before I publish anything claiming it works. The receipts will be in the repo. Target ship date is roughly {V1.5_TARGET_DATE_PLACEHOLDER — pick a real date in late July or August}. The launch playbook is the one I just ran, with three changes based on this retrospective:

- Show HN earlier in the sprint, not later (lesson from the VF launch retro that held up across the second half of the campaign too).
- Six receipt-driven demo posts per launch sprint, not three. Demo IS the marketing. I keep relearning this.
- One LinkedIn hero post and one LinkedIn retro post. Skip the middle. The middle post almost always becomes a process update that adds noise.

**The blog series continues.** Posts 12-18 are already written and the asset packages are ready. I am stretching the publishing window so each post gets its own attention rather than cramming them into the next ten weeks. Plan-of-record is one post every two weeks through the end of Q3, paired with a continuing X drumbeat and the same Mon/Thu LinkedIn cadence. The series-close is also when the longer next-decade verification stack essay drops — the one I have been promising in the consulting-magnet posts.

**Q3 consulting capacity is open.** The campaign generated {CONSULTING_DMS_RECEIVED_PLACEHOLDER} qualified DMs and closed {CONSULTING_ENGAGEMENTS_CLOSED_PLACEHOLDER} engagements. Remaining Q3 capacity is small. The engagements I am best-suited for: engineering leadership at 50-500 person orgs adopting AI coding assistants, Series B-C startups feeling validation pain after enterprise customers ask "how do you verify this code works," and regulated industries where "the AI wrote it and the tests pass" was never going to satisfy auditors.
